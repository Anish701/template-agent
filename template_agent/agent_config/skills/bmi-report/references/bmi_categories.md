# BMI Categories and Classifications

## Standard BMI Ranges

| BMI Range | Category | Health Implications |
|-----------|----------|-------------------|
| < 18.5 | Underweight | May indicate malnutrition or underlying health conditions |
| 18.5–24.9 | Normal | Associated with lowest health risk for most adults |
| 25–29.9 | Overweight | Increased risk of cardiovascular disease and diabetes |
| 30–34.9 | Obese (Class I) | Moderate health risk increase |
| 35–39.9 | Obese (Class II) | Severe health risk increase |
| ≥ 40 | Obese (Class III) | Very severe health risk increase |

## BMI Calculation

**Formula:** BMI = weight (kg) / height² (m²)

**Example:**
- Weight: 70 kg
- Height: 1.75 m
- BMI = 70 / (1.75 × 1.75) = 22.86

## Limitations

BMI does not account for:
- **Muscle mass:** Athletes may have high BMI but low body fat
- **Bone density:** Varies by individual
- **Age:** Different ranges may apply to older adults
- **Sex:** Men typically have more muscle mass than women
- **Ethnicity:** Different populations may have different risk profiles

## Population-Specific Variations

### Asian Populations
Some health organizations recommend modified thresholds:
- Overweight: ≥ 23
- Obese: ≥ 27.5

### Children and Adolescents
BMI percentiles are used instead of fixed ranges (age and sex-specific).

### Older Adults (65+)
Slightly higher BMI (25–27) may be protective against mortality.

## When BMI Should Not Be Used

1. Children under 18 (use growth charts instead)
2. Pregnant or breastfeeding women
3. Competitive athletes with high muscle mass
4. Individuals with muscle-wasting conditions
5. People with edema or fluid retention
